NASA Big Idea Challenge


The Breakthrough, Innovative and Game-changing (BIG) Idea Challenge is an initiative supporting NASA’s Space Technology Mission Directorate’s Game Changing Development Program’s efforts to rapidly develop high-impact technologies for infusion in a broad array of future NASA missions. Of all the groups competing, six are given funding to develop and demonstrate their ideas, and then present at a conference where the ideas are judged.

Our Challenge


Transporting materials to space is challenging and costly, making it essential to maximize utility while minimizing volume. Our challenge was to design, develop, and demonstrate innovative applications of low Size, Weight, and Power inflatable technologies, structures, and systems to support lunar operations. Outside of lunar habitats which have been explored already, any inflatable technology is welcome.

What is METALS


METALS overcomes the limitations of polymer inflatables in harsh lunar environments. Made from welded metal sheets, these flat-packable volumes plastically deform during inflation, retaining shape even if punctured, and offer greater durability. Our focus was on using METALS to store cryogenic fuel essential for sustaining humanity's presence among the stars.

Project Details

Org: Northwestern University STARS Club

Date: January 2024-Present

Client: NASA


As a member of the METALS team under the Northwestern University Space Technology and Rocketry Society, I initially focused on fabricating the first-generation inflatables. This process involved selecting optimal metals for lunar applications, such as steel and aluminum, designing inflatable shapes, and water jetting initial designs. I also took charge of constructing a pressure-testing setup to ensure the safe inflation of METALS modules. The work accomplished with the $150,000 grant is detailed in the poster, presentation, and technical report below. After winning the Artemis Award for the technology with the most potential, we are now exploring the next steps to advance and implement METALS for future applications.
As the project evolved, we outsourced fabrication to professional welders with advanced expertise, allowing me to shift my focus to researching and analyzing the thermal performance of METALS. Given that NASA currently lacks a method to store cryogenic rocket fuel on the Moon, METALS offers a promising solution. To validate this, I analyzed experimental cryogenic data in regolith to measure heat loss and developed passive thermal management methods for the inflatables. These efforts culminated in a design capable of achieving cryogenic storage with energy requirements under 2 watts per cubic meter.
