What are Soft Robots?


Imagine a robot that doesn't just operate alongside us but adapts seamlessly to our movementsâ€”flexible, safe, and responsive. Soft robots utilize flexible materials to create adaptable systems that are safe to interact with and can adapt to challenging environments. The big challenge in the field soft robotics is finding fast, efficient, and compliant ways to move.

What are Handed Shearing Auxetics?

Handed Shearing Auxetics (HSAs) are a fast and efficient way to move a soft robot. HSAs are hollow cylinders with a unique pattern on their surface that enables them to extend and contract when twisted. This mechanism simplifies soft robotic design, but advancing HSAs has been constrained by the limitations of current 3D printing and laser-cutting methods capable of manufacturing them.

What did I do?


I developed a robust method for molding HSAs using 3D-printed molds. This approach simplifies and reduces the cost of HSA manufacturing, enables scalable production, and expands material options to high-performance polymers like TPUs, enhancing their flexibility and strength. These improvements make HSAs more accessible and versatile, broadening their potential for applications in the field of soft robotics".
Project Details

Lab: Robotic Matter Lab

Date: January 2023-Present

Robosoft 2025 Paper: Pending Review


After learning to use the CAD software Rhino with Grasshopper to generate the HSA auxetic pattern, I began constructing molds. After trying various designs including soft molds and two piece molds, I quickly settled onto the five piece mold design pictured below.
This mold is the result of many months of constant iteration, playing with tolerances and print settings to address feature resolution, air bubbles, and flashing from the molding process. While initially FDM printed molds were used, SLA printing enabled finer resolution for scaled down HSAs.

This project is still ongoing, but so far has had great success. I have molded HSAs at a 0.3x scale, which can't be done using the other methods. I have also made HSAs from thermoplastic powder that are highly responsive with less material creep than other materials due to the monolithic structure created via molding vs layer by layer printing.
To characterize the thermoplastic HSAs, I built the two testing rigs in the videos at the top of this page. The first was used to quantify force and velocity of single HSA units using a load cell and camera tracking, while the second was used in quantifying the properties of molded HSAs when used in a soft robotic leg.
A paper summarizing this work has been submitted to the Robosoft 2025 conference, and I look forward to seeing how I can utilize the small, responsive, and much quiter HSAs in the future possibly in a soft robotic fish.
